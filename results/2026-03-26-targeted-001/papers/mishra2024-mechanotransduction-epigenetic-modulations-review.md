# Mechanotransduction and epigenetic modulations of chromatin: Role of mechanical signals in gene regulation

**Authors:** Mishra J, Chakraborty S, Niharika, Roy A, Manna S, Baral T, Nandi P, Patra SK
**Journal:** Journal of Cellular Biochemistry
**Year:** 2024
**PMID:** 38345428
**DOI:** 10.1002/jcb.30531
**Published:** 2024-03-01

## Abstract

Mechanical forces may be generated within a cell due to tissue stiffness, cytoskeletal reorganization, and the changes in the cell's physical surroundings. These changes of forces impose a mechanical tension within the intracellular protein network. Mechanical tension could be released by a series of protein-protein interactions often facilitated by membrane lipids, lectins and sugar molecules and thus generate a type of signal to drive cellular processes, including cell differentiation, polarity, growth, adhesion, movement, and survival. Recent experimental data have accentuated the molecular mechanism of this mechanical signal transduction pathway, dubbed mechanotransduction. Mechanosensitive proteins in the cell's plasma membrane discern the physical forces and channel the information to the cell interior. Cells respond to the message by altering their cytoskeletal arrangement and directly transmitting the signal to the nucleus through the connection of the cytoskeleton and nucleoskeleton before the information despatched to the nucleus by biochemical signaling pathways. Nuclear transmission of the force leads to the activation of chromatin modifiers and modulation of the epigenetic landscape, inducing chromatin reorganization and gene expression regulation. While significant research has been done on the role of mechanotransduction in tumor development and cancer progression/metastasis, the mechanistic basis of force-activated carcinogenesis is still enigmatic.

## Key Findings

- Tissue stiffness (ECM mechanics) → mechanical tension → intracellular protein signaling cascade → chromatin modifiers
- Mechanosensitive plasma membrane proteins gate the force signal into the cell
- Direct nuclear force transmission: cytoskeleton → nucleoskeleton → chromatin modifier activation
- Nuclear force transmission leads to epigenetic landscape modulation
- Role in cancer: force-activated carcinogenesis via epigenetic dysregulation
