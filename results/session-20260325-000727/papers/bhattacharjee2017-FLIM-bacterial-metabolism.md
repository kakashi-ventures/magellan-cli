# Metabolic Fingerprinting of Bacteria by FLIM

**Title:** Metabolic fingerprinting of bacteria by fluorescence lifetime imaging microscopy
**Authors:** Arunima Bhattacharjee, Rupsa Datta, Enrico Gratton, Allon I. Hochbaum
**Year:** 2017
**Journal:** Scientific Reports
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41598-017-04032-w
**PMC:** PMC5473825

## Abstract

Two-photon FLIM is used to create metabolic fingerprints of individual bacteria and populations via NAD(P)H autofluorescence. Five clinically relevant bacterial species are analyzed at single-cell resolution.

## Key Findings

- **Phasor approach to FLIM:** Fit-free technique that maps free-to-bound NAD(P)H ratio without prior knowledge of fluorescent species
- Phasor positions shift with antibiotic exposure, growth phase, and nutrient conditions
- Single-cell resolution: phasor spread reveals heterogeneity within bacterial populations
- **Five species studied:** E. coli, Salmonella, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Bacillus subtilis, Staphylococcus epidermidis
- Free NADH = shorter lifetime (glycolytic/low metabolic); bound NADH = longer lifetime (OXPHOS/active)

## Persister Cell Coverage

**Persister cells NOT directly studied.** The paper mentions persisters in the introduction as slow-growing phenotypes associated with biofilms, but experiments focus on general metabolic states across growth phases and antibiotic responses — not specifically on persister transition dynamics.

## Key Gap Relative to T5 Hypothesis

The T5 hypothesis proposes combining:
1. **Phasor-FLIM** (established here)
2. **Mother-machine microfluidics** (not in this paper — batch cultures used)
3. **Real-time tracking of persister TRANSITION** (not endpoint snapshots)

This paper provides building block #1 but not the longitudinal single-cell tracking of the persister commitment decision. The gap: tracking a cell in real time as it transitions from normal → persister state (decreasing NADH lifetime, shifting phasor position) has not been done.
