## Abstract

In synthetic biology, genetic components are assembled to make transcriptional units, and transcriptional units are assembled into circuits to perform specific and predictable functions of a genetic device. Genetic devices have been described in bacteria, mammalian cell cultures, and small organoids, yet the development of programmable genetic circuits for devices in plants has lagged. Programmable genetic devices require defining the component's quantitative functions. Here, the authors use plant genetic elements with quantitatively characterized transfer functions and developed in silico models to guide their assembly into a genetic device: a toggle switch or mutually inhibitory gene-regulatory device. The approach allows for computational selection of plant genetic components and iterative refinement of the circuit. The computationally selected genetic circuit functions as predicted in stably engineered plants, including through tissue and organ differentiation. Developing abilities to produce predictable and programmable plant genetic devices opens the prospect of predictably engineering plants' unique abilities in sustainable human and environmental systems.

## Key Findings

1. **First stably verified toggle switch in plants**: Previous plant synthetic biology focused on transient expression. This work demonstrates a bistable toggle switch that functions predictably through tissue and organ differentiation in stably engineered plants.

2. **Computational-experimental design loop**: Transfer function characterization → mathematical model → computational circuit selection → experimental verification. Reduced trial-and-error in plant circuit engineering.

3. **Plant-specific challenges addressed**: Long life span, environmental sensitivity, developmental plasticity, and alternation of generations — all validated as compatible with predictable circuit behavior when components are properly characterized.

4. **Toggle switch = bistable epigenetic-like state**: The circuit locks into one of two stable gene expression states and maintains it through cell division and differentiation.

## Cross-Domain Significance

- **Plant SAR as a natural toggle**: Plant systemic acquired resistance (SAR) itself may operate as a natural bistable switch — the H3K4me3/H3K36me2-primed chromatin state functions analogously to a toggle switch set to "ON" after pathogen encounter. Engineering this switch is now feasible in plants.
- **Immune toggle architecture**: The same toggle switch logic applies to histone lactylation (H3K18la) as a bistable switch in macrophages — M1 (anti-inflammatory) vs. M2 (immunosuppressive) states are stabilized by different lactylation patterns. This is a natural Boolean memory element.
- **Synthetic biology × Plant defense**: Could a genetic toggle switch be engineered to rapidly and durably activate plant SAR upon sensing pathogen signals, without the 24-48h induction lag of natural SAR?
- **Synthetic circuits × Cancer immunotherapy**: Toggle switch logic could be applied to reprogram tumor-infiltrating macrophages from M2 (immunosuppressive) to M1 (anti-tumor) by engineering synthetic bistable circuits targeting the H3K18la landscape.
