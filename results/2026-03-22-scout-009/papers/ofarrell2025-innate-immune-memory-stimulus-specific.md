## Key Findings (from abstract/metadata)

1. **Stimulus-specificity of trained immunity**: Innate immune memory (trained immunity) is not a generic "heightened alert" state but is stimulus-specific — the epigenetic reprogramming induced by different training stimuli (β-glucan, BCG, LPS) produces distinct memory signatures.

2. **Implication**: Different pathogens/vaccines produce qualitatively different trained immunity programs, not just quantitatively different ones. The epigenetic landscape differs per training stimulus.

3. **Single-cell resolution**: The study uses high-resolution approaches to distinguish between cell-intrinsic stimulus specificity vs. population heterogeneity.

---

## Cross-Domain Significance

- **Specificity mechanism**: If trained immunity is stimulus-specific, this implies that the epigenetic marks (H3K18la, H3K4me3) are differentially distributed across genomic loci depending on the training stimulus. This means the "toggle switch" model for trained immunity is more nuanced than a simple ON/OFF state — it's a stimulus-specific program.
- **m6A connection**: Does stimulus specificity extend to the epitranscriptome? Are different m6A patterns on immune gene mRNAs established by different training stimuli?
- **Gap**: What determines stimulus specificity? Is it the specific metabolic products (lactate from β-glucan vs. itaconate from LPS) that direct different histone modifications to different genomic loci?
- **Plant SAR analogy**: Plant SAR is also somewhat stimulus-specific (different pathogens induce overlapping but distinct priming programs). Is stimulus specificity a conserved feature of cross-kingdom immune memory?
