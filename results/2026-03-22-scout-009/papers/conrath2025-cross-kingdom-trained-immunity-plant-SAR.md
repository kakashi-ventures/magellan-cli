## Abstract

Plants face constant microbial threats and have evolved highly effective immune systems characterized by inducible defence mechanisms. On recognizing microbial patterns and/or effectors, plants activate localized pattern-triggered immunity and/or effector-triggered immunity, which culminate in systemic acquired resistance — a broad-spectrum immune response that enhances protection throughout the plant. Systemic acquired resistance shares striking similarities with mammalian trained immunity, particularly in defence priming, which equips organisms with an enhanced capacity to respond to subsequent infections. This Review explores the cross-kingdom similarities between systemic acquired resistance and trained immunity, emphasizing their potential to transform agricultural practices and medical therapies. These insights present innovative opportunities for developing new plant-protection strategies, producing disease-resistant crops and optimizing vaccine approaches, while also highlighting critical knowledge gaps to inspire future research.

## Key Findings

1. **Parallel priming mechanisms**: Both plant SAR and mammalian trained immunity involve: (a) systemic signal release (NHP/pipecolic acid in plants; IFN-γ in mammals), (b) transition to a "primed state" of heightened defence readiness, (c) enhanced pattern recognition receptor levels (FLS2 in plants; TLR2 in mammals), (d) elevated MAPK signalling enzymes, (e) metabolic reprogramming.

2. **Pipecolic acid as cross-kingdom metabolite**: Pipecolic acid (Pip) and its derivative N-hydroxy-pipecolic acid (NHP) are the primary systemic signals of plant SAR. Crucially, pipecolic acid has been identified in **mammalian immune metabolism** as a modulator of mTOR signalling, linking early-life exercise to enhanced anti-inflammatory immunity. This cross-kingdom metabolite is a direct bridge candidate.

3. **Epigenetic priming**: Both SAR and trained immunity require epigenetic reprogramming — histone modifications (H3K4me3, H3K36me2) mark "primed" genes in plants, analogous to H3K4me3 and H3K18la in mammalian trained monocytes.

4. **Pattern recognition convergence**: Pattern-triggered immunity (PTI) in plants and toll-like receptor signalling in mammals show evolutionary convergence in downstream signalling (MAPK cascades, Ca²⁺ fluxes, ROS bursts).

5. **Knowledge gaps identified**: The molecular basis of cross-kingdom mechanisms is largely uncharacterised. Specifically: whether plant priming compounds can activate mammalian innate immune memory, and whether mammalian trained immunity signals could enhance plant resistance.

## Critical Unexplored Bridges

- **Pipecolic acid → H3K18la**: Does pipecolic acid (plant SAR signal that acts on mammalian mTOR) also shift monocyte metabolism toward lactate production, thereby inducing histone lactylation as an alternative trained immunity induction route?
- **NHP in mammalian systems**: Zero studies testing whether N-hydroxy-pipecolic acid activates human monocyte trained immunity programmes.
- **Shared histone marks**: Plant SAR uses H3K4me3/H3K36me2 for priming; mammalian trained immunity uses H3K4me3 + H3K18la. Direct comparison of writer/eraser enzymes across kingdoms not done.
- **Cross-application**: Could BCG-type trained immunity principles be applied to engineer crop disease resistance? Could plant SAR pathways inspire new vaccine adjuvants?
