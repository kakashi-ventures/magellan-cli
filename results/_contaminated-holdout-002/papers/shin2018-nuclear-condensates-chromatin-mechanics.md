# Liquid Nuclear Condensates Mechanically Sense and Restructure the Genome

**Authors**: Shin Y, Chang YC, Lee DSW, Berry J, Sanders DW, Ronceray P, Wingreen NS, Haataja M, Brangwynne CP
**Year**: 2018
**Journal**: Cell
**PMID**: 30500535
**PMC**: 6724728
**DOI**: 10.1016/j.cell.2018.10.057
**Status**: Peer-reviewed, foundational paper

---

## Abstract

Phase transitions involving biomolecular liquids are a fundamental mechanism underlying intracellular organization. In the cell nucleus, liquid-liquid phase separation of intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) is implicated in assembly of the nucleolus, as well as transcriptional clusters, and other nuclear bodies. However, it remains unclear whether and how physical forces associated with nucleation, growth, and wetting of liquid condensates can directly restructure chromatin. Here, we use CasDrop, a novel CRISPR-Cas9-based optogenetic technology, to show that various IDPs phase separate into liquid condensates that mechanically exclude chromatin as they grow and preferentially form in low-density, largely euchromatic regions. A minimal physical model explains how this stiffness sensitivity arises from lower mechanical energy associated with deforming softer genomic regions. Targeted genomic loci can nonetheless be mechanically pulled together through surface tension-driven coalescence.

## Key Findings

### Condensate Stiffness Sensitivity
- Phase-separated condensates preferentially nucleate in **euchromatic (softer) regions**
- Mechanism: lower mechanical energy cost to deform softer chromatin
- Condensates **mechanically exclude denser chromatin** as they grow
- Minimal physical model: stiffness sensitivity is intrinsic to the physics of condensate nucleation

### Chromatin Restructuring by Condensates
- Condensates can **mechanically pull targeted genomic loci together** via surface tension-driven coalescence
- This creates de facto chromatin contacts WITHOUT canonical loop extrusion (cohesin/CTCF)
- Nuclear condensates function as "**mechano-active chromatin filters**"
  - Pull in: targeted genomic loci (by condensate surface tension)
  - Push out: non-targeted, mechanically stiffer neighboring chromatin

### Implications for Enhancer Regulation
- Transcriptional condensates (containing YAP, BRD4, MED1) would preferentially form at euchromatic loci
- Condensate growth EXCLUDES nearby heterochromatic/stiff chromatin
- Multiple enhancers in euchromatic regions could be co-recruited into one condensate hub — without chromatin looping
- This provides a mechanistic explanation for looping-independent E-P communication
