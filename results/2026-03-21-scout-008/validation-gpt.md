## H1.2 / H2.2: FDX1 as Calibrated Kinetic Gate

### 1. Novelty Verdict: **CONTESTED**
- **Closest prior work:**

  - Cu-thiol speciation work (e.g. **Xiao & Wedd**) already shows that biological ligands radically reshape Cu redox chemistry.
- **What is novel here:** the idea that FDX1 is a **precisely tuned kinetic gate sitting on a ligand-extended intracellular Cu Pourbaix boundary**.
- **Specific verification**
  1. **Hsiao et al. 2025 Nat Commun / D136-D139**: **NOT FOUND after searching** my 2024-06 corpus for “FDX1 deep mutational scanning D136 D139 cuproptosis”. Treat as **unverified**.
  2. **Chem Eur J 2025 EPR study of FDX1–elesclomol-Cu²⁺**: **NOT FOUND after searching** “FDX1 elesclomol EPR”, “elesclomol ferredoxin direct electron transfer”. Treat as **unverified**.
  3. **Intracellular copper Pourbaix/Eh-pH formalism**: **NOT FOUND after searching** “intracellular copper Pourbaix”, “mitochondrial copper Eh-pH”. Closest prior art is **speciation/conditional stability modeling**, not formal intracellular Pourbaix diagrams.
  4. **FDX1 thermodynamic redundancy paradox**: **NOT FOUND after searching** that exact framing. Indirectly, the literature addresses it by emphasizing **ligand-bound copper trafficking** and **FDX1’s role in lipoylation**, not bulk Cu²⁺/Cu⁺ equilibrium.
  5. **Mechanistic papers on why FDX1 is required**: yes, but they split into **two non-equivalent interpretations**:  
     - FDX1 helps generate toxic **Cu(I)**  
     - FDX1 maintains **LIAS/lipoylation**, thereby creating the vulnerable substrate set  
     This remains **unresolved**.

### 2. Counter-Evidence
- **The redox-boundary derivation appears underdetermined.** To shift a Cu²⁺/Cu⁺ boundary with a ligand, you need the **conditional stability constants for both oxidation states**, not a single Ka.
- **Biological copper is already overwhelmingly Cu(I)-ligated.** The “boundary at -260 mV” may not be the operative biological variable at all.
- **FDX1 KO chronically lowers lipoylation.** That is a major confound: FDX1 dependence may reflect **target availability**, not Cu²⁺ reduction per se.
- **FDX1 redox potential is not a single exact number** across conditions/protein states; matching it to a computed boundary may be coincidental.
- **Elesclomol may deliver copper directly to mitochondria/proteins**, making unloading/trafficking—not redox—the rate-limiting step.
- **Absolute novelty claim is too strong.** Formal intracellular Cu Pourbaix may be new, but **thermodynamic copper speciation in biological thiol environments is not**.

### 3. Mechanism Plausibility: **5/10**
Plausible core: **FDX1 could kinetically accelerate reduction of elesclomol-bound Cu²⁺**.  
Weak part: the claim that it is a **calibrated electrochemical gate centered at -260 ± 30 mV**.

Main issue: the model imports **bulk electrochemical intuition** into a system where **protein binding and thiol complexation dominate**. So the qualitative concept is reasonable; the quantitative precision is not.

### 4. Experimental Design
- **PHREEQC/Pourbaix first:** okay, but only if you use **conditional β values for Cu(I) and Cu(II)** with **GSH, elesclomol, protein thiolates, pH, ionic strength**. A simple aqueous Pourbaix is not enough.
- **FDX1 mutant-library experiment:** interesting, but many mutants will alter:
  - FDXR interaction
  - LIAS interaction
  - protein stability
  - cluster incorporation  
  not just E₀'.
- **XANES on isolated mitochondria at controlled Eh:** very hard. Bulk Eh clamping can perturb mitochondria, and **beam-induced Cu reduction** is a serious artifact.
- **ITC for transfer:** poor choice for such tight, redox-coupled binding. Use **competition kinetics/spectroscopy**, not ITC.

**Minimal viable alternative:**  
1. Reconstitute **FDX1 + FDXR + NADPH + elesclomol-Cu(II)** in vitro.  
2. Monitor **Cu(I) generation** by **BCS/BCA competition, EPR quench, or XAS**.  
3. Compare **FDX1 vs FDX2 vs GSH alone**.  
4. Then test transfer into a **lipoylated DLAT peptide/domain** or LIAS-containing system.  

Best cell test: **acute degron of FDX1 after lipoylation is already established**, then challenge with elesclomol-Cu. If acute FDX1 loss still blocks toxicity before lipoylation decays, that supports a true **kinetic gate** role.

### 5. Final Assessment
- **Original confidence (from MAGELLAN): 7/10**
- **Your updated confidence: 4/10**
- **Change reason:** high novelty, but the **electrochemical derivation is underconstrained**, the **2025 support papers are unverified**, and **FDX1’s lipoylation biology is a strong alternative explanation**.
- **Experimental feasibility: MEDIUM**
- **Recommended next step:** first establish **direct FDX1-catalyzed reduction kinetics of elesclomol-Cu(II)** versus FDX2/GSH in a purified system.

## H1.3: H₂S–CuS Feed-Forward Loop in Mitochondria

### 1. Novelty Verdict: **NOVEL**
- **Closest prior work:** general copper-sulfide chemistry, H₂S biology, and sulfur-rich copper deposits in metal-overloaded tissues; I do **not** see this mechanism proposed for **cuproptosis mitochondria**.
- **Specific verification**
  1. **Endogenous CuS formation in mammalian mitochondria during copper stress**: **NOT FOUND after searching** “mitochondrial copper sulfide mammalian”, “cuproptosis CuS”, “mitochondria copper sulfur deposits”.
  2. **Biphasic H₂S protection then potentiation of copper toxicity**: **NOT FOUND after searching** “H2S biphasic copper toxicity”, “sulfide potentiates copper cytotoxicity”.
  3. **Ion-product check:**  
     - If total sulfide is **10–100 nM**, then free **S²⁻** at pH ~7.4 is tiny but nonzero (roughly **10⁻¹⁵ to 10⁻¹³ M**, depending on pKa₂ used).  
     - For **CuS Ksp ~10⁻³⁶**, precipitation is thermodynamically favored if free **Cu²⁺** exceeds roughly **10⁻²³ to 10⁻²¹ M**.  
     - So **for Cu²⁺**, yes, precipitation can be thermodynamically allowed.  
     - But biologically relevant copper is often **Cu(I)** and tightly ligated; for Cu(I)/Cu₂S-like chemistry, the threshold free Cu(I) is much less forgiving. So this result is **not decisive biologically**.
  4. **CuS + H₂O₂ oxidative dissolution at biological H₂O₂ (10⁻⁸–10⁻⁷ M)**: **NOT FOUND after searching** environmental and bioinorganic literature terms. Most dissolution studies use **µM–mM oxidant**.
  5. **Current view of H₂S in copper toxicity**: mostly **protective/modulatory** (chelation, redox effects, signaling interference), not a known cuproptotic feed-forward amplifier.

### 2. Counter-Evidence
- **Steady-state H₂S is very low** and rapidly consumed by mitochondrial sulfide oxidation pathways (SQOR/ETHE1 axis).
- **NaHS at 100 µM is non-physiologic** relative to 10–100 nM steady-state claims and can itself perturb mitochondria.
- **Copper in cells is tightly buffered**, so the existence of enough free/local copper for CuS nucleation is questionable.
- The likely intracellular product may be **amorphous Cu(I)-thiolate/polysulfide material**, not clean CuS.
- The oxidative dissolution step is weakly supported at **biological peroxide levels**.
- ETC disruption does **not reliably increase mitochondrial H₂O₂** in a sustained way; it can also reduce ROS output.

### 3. Mechanism Plausibility: **3/10**
Thermodynamically, a copper-sulfur deposit is **not impossible**. Biologically, the proposed loop requires too many uncertain conditions to line up:
- enough local free copper,
- enough local sulfide,
- actual deposit formation,
- meaningful re-dissolution at mild matrix acidification/peroxide.

So this is interesting, but presently weak.

### 4. Experimental Design
- **NaHS 100 µM** is a major confound. Use **slow-release donors** or endogenous-pathway genetics instead.
- **Nigericin 10 µM** is also highly confounding for mitochondrial physiology.
- **TEM/EDX** is low-sensitivity for tiny amorphous mito deposits; sample prep can redistribute ions.
- Better approaches:
  - **cryo-XAS/EXAFS**
  - **synchrotron XRF**
  - **NanoSIMS**
  - manipulate **SQOR / 3-MST / CBS-CSE** genetically

**Minimal viable alternative:**  
Use **isolated mitochondria** with physiological GSH, controlled copper loading, and controlled sulfide flux. Measure Cu-S speciation by **XAS/XRF** before and after mild oxidant/pH perturbation. Only if that works should you move to whole-cell biphasic toxicity studies.

### 5. Final Assessment
- **Original confidence (from MAGELLAN): 5/10**
- **Your updated confidence: 3/10**
- **Change reason:** thermodynamics do not rule it out, but there is **no direct precedent**, the **oxidative-dissolution leg is poorly supported**, and the proposed whole-cell reagents are highly confounded.
- **Experimental feasibility: LOW**
- **Recommended next step:** test for **actual Cu-S species in isolated mitochondria** first; do not start with NaHS/nigericin viability curves.
