## Abstract
Comprehensive review of elesclomol as a copper ionophore that selectively targets mitochondrial metabolism through cuproptosis induction. Describes mechanism, selectivity for mitochondria over cytoplasm, and therapeutic implications.

## Key Findings

### Copper Transport Mechanism
- Elesclomol forms 1:1 complex with Cu(II) extracellularly
- Cu(II)-elesclomol complex is membrane-permeable → enters cells
- Selectively accumulates in mitochondria (unlike disulfiram which distributes broadly)
- FDX1 reduces Cu(II) → Cu(I) inside mitochondria
- Cu(I) then binds lipoylated DLAT → cuproptosis

### Speciation-Activity Relationship
- Cu(II)-elesclomol complex: stable, transport-competent, low toxicity
- Cu(I) after FDX1 reduction: directly binds lipoyl moiety, toxic
- The speciation change (Cu2+ → Cu+) is the switch from "transport mode" to "kill mode"
- Conditional stability constant for ELC-Cu(II): 24-fold higher than TRIEN chelator

### Selectivity
- Cancer cells with high mitochondrial activity: highly sensitive
- Glycolytic cells: resistant (less FDX1/lipoylation)
- Mitochondrial stem cells and drug-resistant variants: highly sensitive

### Missing Data
- No half-cell potential (E°) values reported for ELC-Cu2+/ELC-Cu+ couple
- No pH-dependence data for Cu speciation in mitochondrial matrix
- No thermodynamic modeling of Cu species stability across physiological Eh range

## Relevance to Session 008
Illustrates that elesclomol's efficacy depends entirely on the Cu2+→Cu+ speciation transition — a thermodynamically defined redox process. The mitochondrial matrix Eh (~-280 mV, pH 7.5-8.0) falls in a specific region of the copper Pourbaix diagram that has never been plotted for this system. Geochemical speciation modeling could predict which copper species predominate under different mitochondrial metabolic states.
