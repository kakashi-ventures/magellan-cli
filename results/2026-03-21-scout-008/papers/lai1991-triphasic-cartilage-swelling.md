# Paper: A Triphasic Theory for the Swelling and Deformation Behaviors of Articular Cartilage

## Citation
- **Authors:** Lai WM, Hou JS, Mow VC
- **Journal:** Journal of Biomechanical Engineering
- **Year:** 1991
- **PMID:** 1921350
- **DOI:** Key extension of Mow 1980 biphasic theory

## Abstract / Key Findings
Extends biphasic theory to triphasic by adding ionic (electrolyte) phase. Negatively charged proteoglycans fixed to solid matrix create fixed charge density (FCD). Donnan osmotic pressure from ion partitioning drives swelling against collagen tension.

## Key Framework Elements
Three phases:
1. Solid matrix (collagen + proteoglycans, with fixed negative charges)
2. Interstitial fluid
3. Ionic phase (mobile cations/anions)

Donnan osmotic pressure: π = RT·c_f·(1 - 2c_f / √(4I² + c_f²))^(1/2) - I)
where c_f = fixed charge density, I = ionic strength of bathing solution

## Quantitative Values
- FCD of normal cartilage: ~0.1–0.2 mEq/mL extrafibrillar water
- Donnan osmotic pressure at FCD ~0.4 mmol/g and 0.15 M NaCl: ~3 atmospheres (300 kPa)
- This osmotic pressure contributes substantially to aggregate modulus

## Relevance to Biofilm
Alginate biofilms have carboxylate groups (pKa ~3.4–3.7) providing anionic charge similar in origin to uronate residues in cartilage GAGs. Pel is CATIONIC. Psl is neutral. The Donnan framework directly applies to alginate-dominated biofilms but has never been quantitatively applied. The FCD of alginate biofilms has never been measured.

## Why Selected
Provides the triphasic/Donnan framework that could quantitatively connect cartilage physics to charged biofilm polysaccharides. The FCD-osmotic pressure-permeability coupling in this model is exactly what is missing from biofilm mechanics.
