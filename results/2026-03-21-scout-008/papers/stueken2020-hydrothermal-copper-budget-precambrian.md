## Abstract
Models how hydrothermal vent inputs and organic complexation maintained dissolved copper concentrations in Precambrian oceans despite predictions of near-zero Cu under reducing conditions. Shows Cu concentrations of 2.7-3.4 nM were sustained by organic ligand complexation.

## Key Findings

### Copper Speciation Under Different Redox Regimes
- Archean ocean (ferruginous, reducing): hydrothermal vents = major Cu source
- Without organic ligands: Cu precipitates as sulfide minerals (CuS, CuFeS2) proximal to vents
- With organic ligands (thiols, porphyrins): Cu remains in solution as Cu-ligand complexes
- Free Cu2+ at equilibrium: 10^-13 to 10^-15 M (essentially zero without ligands)

### Speciation Framework
- Uses thermodynamic stability constants (Ksp) rather than Pourbaix diagrams
- Ksp = 10^-14 for Cu-organic ligand complexes
- Model predicts dissolved Cu in nM range when organic ligands present
- Copper speciation is entirely controlled by redox potential, pH, and ligand concentration

### Key Analogy to Cuproptosis
- Ancient hydrothermal vent environment: organic thiol ligands prevent copper precipitation
- Biological ligand analogs: ATOX1, COX17, CCS (copper chaperones)
- Both use thiol/sulfhydryl groups for Cu+ binding
- Elesclomol acts as synthetic equivalent of hydrothermal organic Cu-transport ligand

### Geochemical-Biological Connection
- Evolution of copper chaperones (metallochaperones) likely occurred in environments with hydrothermal copper speciation
- The chemistry of copper delivery in cells mirrors the chemistry of copper transport in vent systems
- However: no paper has mapped this parallel using quantitative speciation frameworks

## Relevance to Session 008
Establishes that hydrothermal vent Cu speciation is governed by the same thermodynamic principles as intracellular Cu handling — thiol ligand complexation of Cu+ controls bioavailability. This paper covers the geochemical side; no paper covers the biological (cuproptosis) side with this same rigor.
