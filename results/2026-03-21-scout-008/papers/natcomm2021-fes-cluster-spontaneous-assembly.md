# Spontaneous assembly of redox-active iron-sulfur clusters at low concentrations of cysteine

**Authors**: Wächtershäuser group / multiple
**Journal**: Nature Communications
**Year**: 2021
**DOI**: 10.1038/s41467-021-26158-2
**URL**: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-26158-2
**Status**: Peer-reviewed

## Abstract (reconstructed from search results)

Iron-sulfur (FeS) clusters spontaneously assemble in the presence of cysteine at low concentrations, under conditions similar to those expected in Hadean alkaline hydrothermal vents. The assembled clusters are redox-active. This provides a plausible prebiotic mechanism for the emergence of FeS proteins (including ferredoxins) at the origin of life. CO₂ reduction can be catalyzed by FeS minerals, forming universally conserved carboxylic acids including formate, acetate, and pyruvate, with prebiotic syntheses closely resembling microbial CO₂ reduction via the acetyl-CoA pathway.

## Key Findings

1. **Spontaneous FeS cluster assembly**: At low cysteine concentrations and under mildly reducing alkaline conditions (mimicking Hadean hydrothermal vents), FeS clusters form abiotically. These clusters are redox-active, meaning they can donate/accept electrons.

2. **Conditions: alkaline hydrothermal vent chemistry**: The assembly requires Fe²⁺, sulfide (HS⁻), and cysteine. Temperature range: ambient to ~90°C. pH: mildly alkaline. These conditions match Lost City-type alkaline serpentinite vents, not black smokers.

3. **rTCA pathway link**: The prebiotic FeS chemistry produces acetate and pyruvate — the same metabolites processed by the TCA cycle enzymes (including PDH/DLAT) that are the targets of cuproptosis.

4. **Ferredoxins as the first proteins**: The ferredoxin family's evolutionary debut presumably predates membrane-bound life. Phylogenetic analyses suggest ferredoxins are among the oldest proteins, predating LUCA.

## Relevance to Cross-Field Connection (Critical Bridge Paper)

This paper provides the geochemical anchor for the evolutionary echo hypothesis:
- FeS clusters form spontaneously at alkaline hydrothermal vents → ferredoxins (including FDX1 ancestor) evolved from these mineral clusters
- The same alkaline vent conditions where FeS formed would also have contained ambient Cu²⁺ (from basalt/serpentinite leaching)
- Primitive ferredoxin-like FeS proteins would have reduced Cu²⁺ to Cu⁺ (just as modern FDX1 does) due to the redox potential of FeS clusters (~−300 to −500 mV) being strongly negative relative to Cu²⁺/Cu⁺ (+159 mV)
- The lipoylated TCA enzymes (DLAT ancestor) co-evolved in the same environment → selection pressure to manage Cu⁺ toxicity to lipoyl groups

**The quantitative case**: If alkaline vent Eh is −300 to −500 mV and Cu²⁺/Cu⁺ is +159 mV, the driving force for abiotic Cu²⁺ reduction by FeS at hydrothermal vents is ~460–660 mV. This is LARGER than the modern FDX1-mediated reaction (~430 mV). The biological cuproptosis mechanism is thermodynamically weaker than the ancestral geochemical reaction — consistent with evolution retaining a vestigial but functional echo.
