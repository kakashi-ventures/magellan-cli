## Abstract
First study to systematically examine the role of zinc in controlling aqueous sulfide speciation at deep-sea hydrothermal vents. ZnS precipitation was thermodynamically favored at all sites as vent fluids cooled, reducing free sulfide availability to chemoautotrophic organisms.

## Key Findings

### Speciation Controls on Bioavailability
- ZnS(s) precipitation removes dissolved sulfide from solution
- Cooling and pH increase trigger solid phase precipitation
- High temperature + low pH: dissolved Zn-chloride and Zn-bisulfide complexes dominate
- Solid precipitates: reduced bioavailability to sulfide-reliant organisms

### Thermodynamic Framework Applied
- Authors use thermodynamic stability constants to predict speciation
- Eh-pH conditions determine whether dissolved vs solid phase predominates
- Same framework applies to Cu-sulfide systems (chalcopyrite CuFeS2, covellite CuS)

### Key Analogy
- Fe/Zn sulfide precipitation = Cu sulfide precipitation → both remove metal from bioavailable pool
- CuS (covellite) solubility product (Ksp ~10^-36) means essentially zero free Cu2+ in sulfide-rich vent fluids
- Only organic ligand-complexed Cu (or Cu+ in reduced form) remains in solution

### Relevance to Cu Speciation
- The study directly implies: in Cu-rich hydrothermal systems, similar analysis would apply
- Cu would precipitate as CuS or CuFeS2 unless stabilized by organic ligands
- The "bioavailability window" for Cu is the Eh-pH region where Cu remains dissolved but below toxic threshold

## Relevance to Session 008
Establishes geochemical methodology for speciation-controlled bioavailability. The same analytical framework (thermodynamic stability constants, Eh-pH phase diagrams) that geochemists use to predict metal bioavailability in vent systems has never been applied to predict Cu bioavailability in mitochondria or to understand cuproptosis thresholds.
