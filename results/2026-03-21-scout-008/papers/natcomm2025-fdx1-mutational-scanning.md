## Abstract (reconstructed from search results)

A deep mutational scanning (DMS) approach was used to systematically map which residues of FDX1 are required for its two known functions in cuproptosis: (1) reduction of Cu²⁺ to Cu⁺ and (2) regulation of protein lipoylation. The study identified two conserved solvent-exposed residues, D136 and D139, located on alpha-helix 3, as essential for both functions. These residues are distinct from the Fe-S cluster binding cysteines and the ferredoxin reductase interaction surface, suggesting that the Cu²⁺-reduction and lipoylation activities of FDX1 involve a dedicated protein surface patch separate from its canonical electron-transfer interface.

## Key Findings

1. **D136 and D139 are essential for cuproptosis AND lipoylation**: Mutation of either residue abolishes both activities. These are solvent-exposed aspartates on helix 3.

2. **FDX1 vs FDX2 specificity explained**: FDX2 (33% sequence identity with FDX1) cannot support cuproptosis despite having the same [2Fe-2S] cluster and ferredoxin reductase interaction capability. The DMS data maps this specificity to the D136/D139 region and surrounding helix-3 surface.

3. **The Fe-S cluster is necessary but not sufficient**: Residues required for Fe-S cluster binding (canonical cysteines) are essential for function, but Fe-S cluster integrity alone does not explain cuproptosis specificity between FDX1 and FDX2.

4. **Structural implications**: The D136/D139 surface patch may be the direct interface for elesclomol-Cu²⁺ binding or for interaction with lipoylation machinery (LIAS/LIPT1). This distinguishes the Cu-reductase activity from the canonical P450-electron-donor activity.

## Relevance to Cross-Field Connection

- The structural specificity at D136/D139 (acidic residues, solvent-exposed) is compatible with Cu²⁺ coordination — aspartate carboxylates can act as Cu ligands
- If D136/D139 directly coordinates Cu²⁺ before reduction, the FDX1 active site would represent a protein-based Cu²⁺ coordination environment analogous to mineral surfaces at hydrothermal vents where Fe²⁺ reduces Cu²⁺
- The fact that FDX2 lacks this specificity despite identical Fe-S chemistry suggests the Cu-reductase function was a separately evolved addition to the ancestral Fe-S electron transfer function — consistent with the evolutionary echo hypothesis
