## Abstract
Demonstrates that complement system activation (part of innate immunity) follows percolation theory: there is a sharp threshold for complement activation governed by the surface density of potential complement attachment points. Below threshold, complement does not spread; above threshold, a cascade coats the surface. This percolation transition is demonstrated across scales from single nanoparticles to macroscale bacterial surfaces.

## Key Findings
- Complement activation is a percolation-type criticality threshold in surface protein spacing
- Mathematics of percolation (universality class) correctly predicts experimental results
- Positive feedback loop: C3b generates more C3b before degradation creates cascade
- Tested on: nanoparticles, bacteria, DNA origami — all show percolation threshold behavior
- Energy-efficient decision mechanism: "produces complement only where/when needed"
- Analogy: spacing between trees determines whether forest fire spreads
