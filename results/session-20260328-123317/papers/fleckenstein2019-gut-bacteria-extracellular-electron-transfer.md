## Abstract / Key Content

Demonstrates in vivo extracellular electron transfer (EET) in mammalian gut. Key findings:

- EET genes (flavin-based) found across Firmicutes — major gut phylum
- Cyclic voltammetry directly inserted into mouse cecum detected characteristic catalytic wave
- Catalytic wave present in conventional mice, absent in germ-free mice
- Confirmed in rat and guinea pig gut in vivo
- Clostridium cochlearium highest electroactivity; Lactobacillus reuteri and Staphylococcus xylosus also electroactive
- EET couples with fermentation (not just anaerobic respiration) in gut context
- EET activity correlates with metabolic rate (drops dramatically at lower temperatures)

## Significance for T6

This is the key mechanistic paper enabling T6: gut bacteria DO transfer electrons electrochemically in vivo. The EET signal (catalytic wave, charge transfer resistance) reflects metabolic activity. This creates the physical basis for EIS-based metabolic monitoring — but no paper has yet developed EIS equivalent circuit models that capture COMMUNITY-LEVEL metabolic state as a function of microbiome composition and dysbiosis.

## Gap for T6

EET exists in gut bacteria. EIS can detect charge transfer resistance (metabolic proxy). But:
1. No EIS frequency-domain fingerprint of gut microbiome metabolic state established
2. No equivalent circuit model linking Randles parameters to specific community composition or dysbiosis markers
3. No paper distinguishes healthy vs. dysbiotic microbiome by EIS signature
