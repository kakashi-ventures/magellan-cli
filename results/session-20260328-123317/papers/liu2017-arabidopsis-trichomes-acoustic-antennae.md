## Abstract
Investigates whether Arabidopsis thaliana leaf trichomes function as mechanical detectors for vibrations produced by feeding caterpillars. Uses computational modeling to test whether trichome structures resonate at frequencies matching insect feeding sounds.

## Key Findings
- First vibrational mode of archetypal trichome: approximately 6-8 kHz (torsional oscillation of stalk)
- Frequency range matches acoustic emissions from Pieris rapae caterpillar feeding (<8 kHz)
- Higher modes: flexural vibrations of stalk branches (upper audible to ultrasonic range)
- Young's modulus gradient from base to tip determines resonant properties
- pH changes (e.g., from insect saliva) alter Young's modulus and thus resonant frequency
- Trichome mechanical properties modeled from cellulose microfibril anisotropy

## Signal Processing Note
The paper does NOT invoke filter bank theory, matched filter theory, or classical signal processing frameworks. The frequency selectivity is described entirely in mechanical/biomechanical terms. However, the biological phenomenon (a physical structure tuned to resonate at specific ecologically-relevant frequencies) is structurally isomorphic to a mechanical bandpass filter.

## Relevance to T2 (Acoustic Filter-Bank × Plant Bioacoustics)
Establishes the mechanistic basis for the T2 bridge: trichomes as tuned mechanical resonators. The paper provides the physical grounding for the proposed analogy to matched-filter detection. Missing from this paper: (1) explicit connection to filter-bank engineering theory, (2) ensemble diversity analysis (multiple trichome sizes as a bank of filters), (3) MSL/MCA channel gating as frequency-selective transducer.
