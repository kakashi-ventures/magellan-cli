## Abstract
Investigates whether high-density collagen in tumors influences T cell activity using 3D culture systems. Demonstrates that high collagen density (4 mg/ml vs. 1 mg/ml) impairs T cell proliferation and cytotoxic function, and promotes regulatory phenotype.

## Key Findings
- T cell proliferation significantly reduced in 4 mg/ml vs 1 mg/ml collagen (binary comparison)
- High-density collagen downregulates cytotoxic markers (perforin, granzyme B)
- Upregulates regulatory T cell markers under high density
- Mechanism: autocrine TGF-beta signaling activated by matrix mechanics
- Motile T cells found mainly in loose ECM stromal regions; fibrotic areas devoid of lymphocytes
- T cells strongly decelerate when a threshold in stiffness is reached

## Threshold Note
The paper mentions "a threshold in stiffness" at which T cell motility collapses — but this is described qualitatively, without quantitative percolation analysis. The study uses a binary comparison (low vs. high density) rather than scanning across a density gradient to identify a critical transition point.

## Relevance to T1
Establishes the biological phenomenon: collagen density is a threshold-like regulator of T cell function. The T1 target proposes that this threshold corresponds to the percolation transition in the ECM network — a specific quantitative framework that this paper does NOT provide. The percolation interpretation of the stiffness threshold is the novel contribution.
