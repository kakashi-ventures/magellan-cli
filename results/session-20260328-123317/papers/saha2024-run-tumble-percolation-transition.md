# Site-Percolation Transition of Run-and-Tumble Particles

**Authors:** Soumya K. Saha, Aikya Banerjee, P. K. Mohanty
**Institution:** Indian Institute of Science Education and Research Kolkata
**Journal:** Soft Matter (2024)
**DOI:** 10.1039/d4sm00838c
**PubMed:** PMID 39575656
**arXiv:** arXiv:2406.11726

## Abstract Summary
Studies percolation transitions of run-and-tumble particles (RTPs) on a 2D square lattice. Discovers re-entrant phase behavior: particle clusters form at intermediate diffusion rates but dissolve at higher rates. Critical exponents vary continuously along the transition line but scaling function is universal.

## Key Findings
- **Re-entrant percolation**: Two transitions occur as positional diffusion rate p increases: mixed→phase-separated state at p_c = 0.029, then phase-separated→mixed again at p_c = 0.235
- **Universality**: Scaling function is invariant along the critical line — identical to percolation transition in the Ising model
- **Critical exponents vary**: 1/ν ranges from ~1.10 to 1.43 across conditions; β/ν from ~0.05 to 0.14; γ/ν from ~1.90 to 1.73
- **Low-activity limit**: Exponents approach Z₂-percolation class (passive particles)
- **Activity effect**: Activity fundamentally reshapes the phase diagram; does NOT simply shift a passive threshold

## Physical Framework
- Run-and-tumble particles: run toward nearest neighbor along orientation with unit rate; tumble with rate ω to change orientation
- Model is relevant to bacteria-like self-propelled particles
- 2D square lattice — biological systems are 3D (different thresholds expected)

## Relevance to Session 015
- **Critical confirmation**: Active particles (like T cells) do NOT behave like passive percolants — the percolation threshold is shifted and the critical exponents differ
- **Active particle correction needed**: T cells have ~30 µm/min peak speed, run-and-tumble-like behavior with pauses and direction changes at obstacles — more complex than purely passive diffusion
- **Open question**: What are the critical exponents for an active particle analogue of T cells in 3D fibrous ECM? This is NOT established by this paper
- **The paper is 2D only**: 3D active percolation in a realistic collagen-like disordered network has not been studied
