## Abstract
Develops a theoretical model examining how acoustic radiation forces affect elongated plant hair cells (trichomes) in tomato plants (Solanum lycopersicum). Proposes that acoustic radiation force may be sufficient to trigger stretch-activated ion channels (including Piezo channels) in trichome membranes.

## Key Findings
- Frequency range analyzed: 1–200 kHz (animal vocalizations, insect ultrasound, bat sonar)
- Mechanism: acoustic waves exert radiation pressure that bends cylindrical hair cells, generating strain at stretch-activated ion channels
- Perturbation theory + finite element simulation used to compute forces
- Hemiptera (sucking insects) may produce signals sufficient to activate Piezo channels
- Stresses from acoustic radiation are low compared to wind, but frequency and repetition may combine with ion channel dynamics
- Different from vibration-mediated trichome deflection: this is steady radiation force

## Relevance to T2 (Acoustic Filter-Bank × Plant Bioacoustics)
Establishes that acoustic radiation force (a frequency-dependent phenomenon) can gate ion channels in trichomes. This supports the T2 hypothesis that different trichome sizes transduce different frequencies to different downstream channels. However, no engineering filter theory is applied, and the MSL/MCA channel specificity for frequency bands is not explored. Piezo channels are proposed, not MSL/MCA specifically.

## Key Gap
No connection between: (1) which specific mechanosensitive channel family is activated, (2) whether different trichome sizes selectively gate different channel types, (3) whether this constitutes a biological matched-filter system in the engineering sense.
