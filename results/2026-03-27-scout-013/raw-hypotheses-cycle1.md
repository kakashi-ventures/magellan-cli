### Hypothesis 1: GEV Tail Index (xi) as a Phylogenomic Signature of Thermal Adaptation Strategy — Mean-Shift vs. Tail-Sculpting in Thermophile, Mesophile, and Psychrophile Proteomes

**Connection**: Extreme value statistics (GEV shape parameter estimation) --> Fisher-Tippett-Gnedenko domain-of-attraction classification of per-species Tm distributions --> Proteome-wide thermal stability distributions (Meltome Atlas cross-species comparison)

**Mechanism**:

[GROUNDED: Jarzab et al. 2020, Nature Methods] The Meltome Atlas provides Tm values for ~48,000 proteins across 13 species spanning archaea to humans, with optimal growth temperatures (OGTs) ranging from ~4C (psychrophiles) to ~80C (hyperthermophiles). The current literature characterizes thermal adaptation exclusively by shifts in mean Tm: thermophiles have higher mean Tm than mesophiles. [GROUNDED: Jarzab et al. 2020, Nature Methods — explicit finding] This mean-centric description ignores the SHAPE of the Tm distribution's lower tail, which determines thermal vulnerability. [PARAMETRIC] The Fisher-Tippett-Gnedenko (FTG) theorem guarantees that the distribution of block minima (negated block maxima) from any sufficiently large i.i.d. sample converges to one of three universal forms, unified as the Generalized Extreme Value (GEV) distribution with shape parameter xi. For Tm distributions, which are bounded below (~0-20C for viable proteins), we expect xi < 0 (Weibull domain of attraction, finite lower endpoint). The key testable claim is that thermal adaptation involves NOT merely shifting the mean Tm upward, but specifically sculpting the LEFT TAIL: thermophile proteomes should exhibit MORE NEGATIVE xi (sharper lower-tail cutoff, fewer dangerously low-Tm outliers relative to the mean) compared to mesophiles, while psychrophiles should show LESS NEGATIVE xi (fatter lower tail, more proteins near the thermal vulnerability edge).

[PARAMETRIC] To test this, fit GEV(mu, sigma, xi) to the negated lower tail (i.e., the block minima over KEGG pathway blocks — approximately 300 blocks per species) for each of the 13 Meltome Atlas species. The GEV maximum likelihood estimation requires ~25 block minima for stable parameter estimation [PARAMETRIC: Coles 2001, An Introduction to Statistical Modeling of Extreme Values, Springer — standard reference but cannot confirm exact page for the 25-block claim; downgrading from GROUNDED]. With ~300 KEGG pathways per species, this is 12x over-powered. The predicted phylogenetic pattern is: xi_thermophile < xi_mesophile < xi_psychrophile (all negative, but thermophiles more negative). This would mean that thermophiles have not only shifted their proteome Tm upward (mean Tm increases with OGT, known) but have ALSO narrowed the lower tail to eliminate thermal vulnerabilities — a distinct evolutionary strategy invisible to mean/SD analysis. A secondary prediction: the GEV location parameter mu should track linearly with OGT (reflecting the known mean shift), but the shape parameter xi should show a NON-LINEAR relationship with OGT, revealing a qualitatively different adaptation strategy in extreme thermophiles vs. moderate mesophiles.

**Multi-level abstraction**:
- *Molecular/Physical*: Individual protein Tm values determined by folding free energy DeltaG_fold; lower-tail proteins have marginal thermodynamic stability.
- *Systemic*: The proteome is a system where the weakest-link (lowest Tm essential protein) determines organism-level thermal failure. Evolutionary pressure acts not just on the mean but on eliminating extreme outliers in the lower tail.
- *Formal/Mathematical*: The GEV shape parameter xi is a universal classifier (FTG theorem) — it captures tail behavior regardless of the underlying distribution family. The hypothesis predicts that xi is an evolutionary variable, not a constant across proteomes.
- *Informational*: This is a bias-variance tradeoff: thermophiles invest in reducing variance (tighter tail, fewer outliers) at the cost of flexibility; psychrophiles tolerate variance (broader tail) to maintain function at low temperatures. The information-theoretic question is whether the tail shape encodes the organism's "thermal error tolerance."

**Confidence**: 6/10 — The Meltome Atlas data exists and GEV fitting is computationally straightforward. The FTG theorem guarantees convergence. The uncertainty is whether xi differences between species are large enough to detect given the sample sizes (~3,700 proteins/species), and whether the xi-OGT relationship is non-linear as predicted. The computational validation estimates SE(xi) ~ 0.016 per species, sufficient to detect differences >0.033 — but the EXPECTED magnitude of xi differences between thermophiles and psychrophiles is unknown.

**Groundedness**: HIGH — The data source (Meltome Atlas, Jarzab 2020, Nature Methods), the mathematical framework (FTG theorem, GEV fitting), and the sample size calculations are all well-established. The specific prediction (xi varies systematically with OGT) is parametric but directly testable.

(2) The independence assumption is violated by protein complex co-aggregation (TPCA shows >350 human complexes with correlated Tm). Even with block maxima over KEGG pathways, intra-pathway correlations from shared complexes could bias xi estimates. (3) The 13 species in the Meltome Atlas may not span a sufficient OGT range — most are mesophiles, with only 1-2 true thermophiles — potentially under-powering the OGT-xi regression.

### Hypothesis 2: Complex-Minimum Tm as an EVT Variable Identifies Thermal Bottleneck Complexes Invisible to Mean-Tm Analysis

The GPD tail parameters (scale sigma_u and shape xi) then characterize the thermal vulnerability tail of the cellular complex machinery.

[PARAMETRIC] This approach solves two problems simultaneously. The biological prediction about conserved bottleneck complexes is testable with existing data. **Groundedness**: HIGH — Data source (Meltome Atlas), complex co-aggregation (TPCA, Mateus 2020, Science), proteasome subunit Tm heterogeneity (Jarzab 2020), and GPD/POT methodology are all literature-grounded. The specific prediction (conserved bottleneck complexes across species) and the return level interpretation are parametric extensions.

(3) Some complexes may be stabilized by inter-subunit interactions such that the in-complex Tm of the weakest subunit is higher than its isolated Tm — the Meltome Atlas measures apparent Tm in lysate, which includes some complex context but may not fully capture cooperative stabilization.

### Hypothesis 3: Censored GEV Estimation Recovers the "Invisible" 20% of the Proteome Below the TPP Measurement Window — A Methodological Bridge from Flood Frequency Analysis to Proteomics

[PARAMETRIC] In hydrology, flood frequency analysis routinely encounters the identical problem: historical floods below a measurement threshold (e.g., before a gauging station was installed) are left-censored. The standard solution is the censored GEV likelihood [PARAMETRIC: attributed to Smith 1985, but the specific publication is "Maximum likelihood estimation in a class of nonregular cases," Biometrika 72(1): 67-90 — this establishes the statistical theory, though the specific application to flood censoring is distributed across multiple hydrology papers; tagging as PARAMETRIC for the flood-specific application]. The censored log-likelihood function replaces the density f(x) for each censored observation with the cumulative probability F(c), where c is the censoring threshold (here, 30C). This allows the GEV parameters (mu, sigma, xi) to be estimated from a mixture of observed Tm values (30-90C) and known-censored observations (Tm < 30C, count known from MS detection at lowest temperature).

[PARAMETRIC] The specific methodological contribution is: (a) Fit censored GEV to the Meltome Atlas data for each species, treating the ~10% of proteins with Tm < 30C as left-censored at the 30C boundary. A second prediction: the identities of the most vulnerable proteins (lowest Tm_predicted) recovered by censored GEV will be enriched for intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) and proteins with known thermal sensitivity annotations — providing biological validation of the statistical extrapolation.

Proteome Tm distributions may not be well-approximated by GEV in the tails, making the extrapolation unreliable. The cross-validation experiment (extending TPP to 20C) is technically feasible but has never been done specifically for this purpose.

**Groundedness**: MEDIUM — The 20% unmeasured problem is well-documented (Jarzab 2020, Figueroa-Navedo 2024). Censored GEV methodology exists in hydrology. **Why this might be WRONG**: (1) Proteome Tm distributions may not be in the GEV domain of attraction for the lower tail — if the true distribution of low-Tm proteins is multi-modal (e.g., a distinct population of IDPs with Tm < 20C and a separate population of marginally stable folded proteins with Tm ~ 25-35C), the GEV fit would mischaracterize the tail shape and produce inaccurate extrapolations. ### Hypothesis 6: The Extremal Index of Proteome Tm Distributions Quantifies the Degree of Thermal Cooperativity in the Proteome — A Universal Measure of "Thermal Coupling"

**Connection**: Extreme value statistics (extremal index theta estimation from clustered exceedances) --> Quantification of thermal cooperativity as departure from independence --> Proteome-wide thermal co-aggregation detected by TPCA

**Mechanism**:

[GROUNDED: Mateus et al. 2020, Science 367:eaaz5268; Lim et al. 2023, Nature Communications — TPCA methodology and >350 human complex annotations] TPCA demonstrates that protein complex subunits co-aggregate: when one subunit denatures, its partners follow because they lose stabilizing inter-subunit contacts. This creates CLUSTERS of thermal denaturation events in the proteome Tm distribution — proteins that denature together at similar temperatures. [PARAMETRIC] In extreme value theory, departures from independence are quantified by the extremal index theta, where theta = 1 indicates perfect independence and theta < 1 indicates clustering of extreme events. The extremal index was formalized by Leadbetter (1983) [PARAMETRIC: attributed to Leadbetter, Lindgren, and Rootzen, "Extremes and Related Properties of Random Sequences and Processes," Springer — a textbook, not a single 1983 paper; the concept predates it; tagging as PARAMETRIC for the specific reference]. In hydrology and meteorology, theta is estimated from runs of exceedances: if extreme temperatures cluster in heat waves rather than occurring independently, theta < 1. The direct analogy: in the proteome Tm distribution, if thermally vulnerable proteins cluster (because they are complex co-subunits), the extremal index of the lower tail will be theta < 1. The estimated value theta provides a single scalar that quantifies the degree of "thermal coupling" in the proteome.

[PARAMETRIC] The specific proposal is: (a) For each species in the Meltome Atlas, sort proteins by Tm and identify lower-tail exceedances below a threshold (e.g., 40C). (b) Estimate theta using the runs estimator or the intervals estimator (both standard in EVT software packages like the R `extRemes` or Python `pyextremes` packages). (c) Compare theta across species. The hypothesis predicts that organisms with more extensive protein complex networks (eukaryotes, especially mammals with >1,000 CORUM-annotated complexes) will have LOWER theta (more clustering) than organisms with simpler proteomes (prokaryotes, especially extremophiles that may have selected for thermally independent proteins to prevent catastrophic cascade failure). This would mean that eukaryotic proteomes are more "thermally cooperative" — thermal failure is a COLLECTIVE event involving clusters of co-destabilizing proteins — while extremophile proteomes are more "thermally modular" — individual proteins fail independently. A specific quantitative prediction: human proteome theta ~ 0.4-0.6 (strong clustering from ~1,000+ complexes); T. thermophilus theta ~ 0.8-0.9 (near-independence, reflecting evolutionary pressure for thermal modularity). The theta values can be cross-validated by computing theta from TPCA-identified complex subunit Tm correlation data — they should agree within statistical uncertainty.

**Confidence**: 5/10 — The extremal index concept is well-defined and estimation methods are mature. The TPCA data on complex co-aggregation provides independent validation. The uncertainty is whether the clustering in Tm data is strong enough for theta estimation to be reliable — if the Tm overlap between different complexes is large (many complexes have similar mean Tm), theta estimation may be confounded by coincidental rather than causal clustering.

**Groundedness**: MEDIUM — TPCA complex co-aggregation is well-grounded (Mateus 2020, Science). The extremal index concept and estimation methods are standard in EVT. The specific prediction about eukaryote vs. prokaryote theta, and the quantitative prediction (theta ~ 0.4-0.6 for humans), are parametric. The claim that extremophiles select for thermal modularity (high theta) is speculative but consistent with the known evolutionary pressure to prevent cascade failure at high temperatures.

**Why this might be WRONG**: (1) The extremal index may not be estimable from Tm data because Tm is a STATIC measurement (one value per protein), not a time series. Classical theta estimation assumes temporal clustering of extreme events. Adapting it to a cross-sectional distribution (ordered by Tm, not time) requires reinterpreting "clustering" as spatial proximity in Tm space, which may not map cleanly to the time-series-derived estimators. (2) Coincidental Tm overlap (many proteins happen to have Tm ~ 45C without being in the same complex) could inflate apparent clustering and deflate theta, making it a poor measure of true thermal cooperativity. (3) The prokaryote/eukaryote prediction assumes that complex network size is the dominant determinant of theta, but other factors (genome-wide expression coordination, metabolic coupling) could also cause clustering.

**Literature gap it fills**: The gap analysis does not explicitly mention the extremal index, but it identifies the protein complex co-aggregation issue as a key concern that needs a principled statistical treatment. The extremal index provides exactly this: a formal measure of departure from independence that bridges the EVT framework (Field A) with the TPCA co-aggregation phenomenon (Field C).
