## Background

**Field A — Extreme Value Statistics**: A branch of probability theory that studies the statistical behavior of extremes (maxima or minima) of large datasets. Key tools:
- **Generalized Extreme Value (GEV) distribution**: Three-parameter family that describes block maxima/minima. The shape parameter ξ classifies tail behavior: ξ < 0 = Weibull domain (bounded tail), ξ = 0 = Gumbel (exponential tail), ξ > 0 = Fréchet (heavy/unbounded tail).
- **Peaks-over-Threshold (POT)**: Fits a Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD) to all observations exceeding a threshold. Parameters: scale σ (spread of tail), shape ξ (tail heaviness).
- **Return levels**: The value exceeded with probability p in a given block. Used in hydrology to predict 100-year floods.
- **Fisher-Tippett-Gnedenko theorem**: Mathematical theorem proving that block maxima of any distribution converge to one of the three GEV families. Analogous in status to the Central Limit Theorem.

**Field C — Proteome Thermal Stability**: The Meltome Atlas (Jarzab et al. 2020, Nature Methods) measured melting temperatures (Tm) for ~48,000 proteins across 13 species using Thermal Proteome Profiling (TPP). Tm is the temperature at which 50% of a protein is denatured. The dataset covers Tm values from ~30°C to ~90°C; proteins with Tm < 30°C or > 90°C are outside the measurement window (roughly 20% of the proteome). The dataset is publicly available at PRIDE accession PXD011929.

## HYPOTHESIS 1: GEV Shape Parameter as a Cross-Species Signature of Thermal Adaptation

### The Idea

Different organisms have evolved distinct strategies for surviving at their native temperatures. This hypothesis proposes that the shape parameter ξ of the Generalized Extreme Value distribution — fitted to each organism's proteome melting temperature data — can distinguish these strategies and correlates with each organism's optimal growth temperature (OGT).

### Proposed Mechanism

Two thermal adaptation strategies predict different ξ signatures:

**Strategy 1 — Tail truncation** (thermophilic organisms): Organisms adapted to sustained high temperatures eliminated their most vulnerable proteins via amino acid substitutions that raise the stability floor (denser hydrophobic cores, more salt bridges, more disulfide bonds). This compresses the lower tail of the Tm distribution, producing more negative ξ (deeper into the Weibull domain, meaning the distribution has a harder lower cutoff).

**Strategy 2 — Distribution shift** (cold-adapted organisms): Cold-adapted organisms shifted their entire Tm distribution leftward to maintain conformational flexibility, without specifically truncating the vulnerable lower tail. This keeps the relative tail shape similar, producing ξ closer to zero (approaching Gumbel behavior).

Mesophiles should be intermediate.

### Quantitative Prediction

Fitting GEV to block minima (minimum Tm per ~300 KEGG pathway blocks per species) across 13 Meltome Atlas species should show: ξ negatively correlates with OGT. Estimated precision: SE(ξ) ≈ 0.029 at n = 5,000–7,000 block observations. Expected ξ difference between thermophilic and psychrophilic organisms: 0.3–0.5 units.

Please verify this SE estimate by computing it in code using standard GEV MLE asymptotic theory.

### Known Weaknesses (from prior evaluation)

- Phylogenetic confounding: archaea vs. eukaryotes differ in proteome composition for reasons beyond thermal adaptation
- Proteome composition (fraction of IDPs, membrane proteins, large complexes) may dominate Tm distribution shape more than OGT does
- Bimodal Tm distributions in some species (e.g., E. coli has two populations) may complicate GEV fitting

## HYPOTHESIS 2: Return Levels of Complex-Minimum Melting Temperature Predict Pathway-Specific Thermal Failure

### The Idea

Each protein complex has a "thermal bottleneck" — the subunit with the lowest melting temperature. When this subunit denatures, the whole complex fails. Return level estimation, borrowed from hydrology (where it predicts 100-year floods), can be applied to the distribution of these bottleneck temperatures to predict the temperature at which a specific cellular process (translation, respiration, etc.) will fail.

### Proposed Mechanism

**Step 1 — Identify bottleneck subunits**: For each annotated protein complex (from CORUM database, ~5,000 human complexes), find the minimum Tm among essential subunits using Meltome Atlas data.

**Step 2 — Fit GPD to complex-minimum Tm distribution**: Using Peaks-over-Threshold, fit a Generalized Pareto Distribution to the lower tail of complex-minimum Tm values (below a threshold u = 45°C).

**Step 3 — Compute return levels**: The p-return level R_p gives the temperature exceeded by fraction p of complexes — equivalently, R_p is the temperature at which fraction p of pathway complexes have lost their bottleneck subunit. Using the standard return level formula from extreme value theory (Coles 2001).

**Multi-level prediction**: The 1% return level of the ribosomal complex-minimum Tm distribution predicts the temperature at which 90% of translation capacity is lost (measurable by puromycin incorporation assay). Similarly for respiratory chain complexes (Seahorse respirometry measures oxygen consumption rate). Predicted agreement: within ±2°C of experimentally measured failure temperature.

### The Bottleneck Evidence

Thermal Proximity Co-aggregation (TPCA) experiments show that proteins within the same complex co-aggregate with intra-complex Tm correlation r = 0.75–0.83, confirming that complex subunits behave as coupled thermal units. This supports using the minimum-Tm subunit as the rate-limiting bottleneck.

### Known Weaknesses (from prior evaluation)

- Chaperones (HSP70, HSP90) may rescue bottleneck subunits in vivo above their in vitro Tm, making predictions systematically optimistic by 3–5°C
- The in vitro lysate Tm from TPP may not reflect in vivo stability (chaperones are diluted in lysates)
- Some complexes have dispensable subunits — losing the lowest-Tm subunit may not inactivate the complex if it is dispensable
- CORUM complex annotations are incomplete outside human/yeast, limiting cross-species application

## HYPOTHESIS 3: GPD Scale Parameter Predicts Evolutionary Rate of Thermally Vulnerable Proteins

### The Idea

The spread of melting temperatures within the thermally vulnerable subproteome — captured by the scale parameter σ of the Generalized Pareto Distribution — encodes how much evolutionary constraint acts on those proteins. A narrow spread means tight constraints and slow evolution; a wide spread means tolerance and faster evolution.

### Proposed Mechanism

**Define the vulnerable subproteome**: Using Peaks-over-Threshold, fit a GPD to proteins in the lower 5th percentile of Tm (the most thermally unstable). The GPD scale parameter σ describes how spread out these proteins' Tm values are.

**The evolutionary logic**:
- Small σ (proteins cluster near a similar low Tm) → any mutation reducing Tm pushes a protein below a critical functional threshold → strong purifying selection → low dN/dS (ratio of non-synonymous to synonymous substitution rates, a standard measure of evolutionary constraint).
- Large σ (proteins span a wide Tm range in the tail) → more tolerance for Tm-reducing mutations → weaker purifying selection → higher dN/dS.

**Testable prediction**: σ (from GPD fit to lower-tail exceedances) negatively correlates with mean dN/dS of tail-protein orthologs across the 13 Meltome Atlas species. Analysis uses PAML codeml for dN/dS estimation, reciprocal best BLAST for ortholog identification, with protein size, disorder content, and expression level as covariates.

### Supporting Citations

1. **Coles 2001, Springer** — GPD methodology
2. **Yang 2007, Molecular Biology and Evolution** — PAML 4 (dN/dS estimation)
3. **Drummond et al. 2005, PNAS 102:14338** — Expression level predicts evolutionary rate

**Citation note**: The hypothesis originally cited Drummond et al. 2005 as "Cell" — the correct journal is PNAS. Please verify.

4. **Leuenberger et al. 2017, Science 355:eaai7825** — Large proteins are less thermostable than small proteins; expression level correlates with stability. This is a key potential confounder.
5. **Jarzab et al. 2020, Nature Methods 17:495–503** — Meltome Atlas

### Known Weaknesses (from prior evaluation)

- dN/dS is dominated by expression level, interaction degree, and essentiality — the σ-dN/dS signal may be undetectable against these confounders
- The identity of thermally vulnerable proteins likely differs across species, making ortholog-based comparisons problematic (if the same genes are not in the tail across species, the comparison breaks down)
- Leuenberger et al. 2017 showed that protein size strongly predicts Tm; if vulnerable proteins are systematically large, σ may reflect size distribution rather than evolutionary constraint
- The GPD scale parameter σ is sensitive to threshold choice; small threshold changes could qualitatively alter σ
- With only 13 species, statistical power for the σ-dN/dS correlation may be insufficient
