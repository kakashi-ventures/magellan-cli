## E1: Abiotic Fenton Regioselectivity Fingerprint as Evidence for Evolutionary Refinement of 15-LOX
**Lineage**: H2 (Ferrihydrite PLOOH Fingerprinting) → SPECIFICATION
**Key improvement**: Replaced falsified r>0.9 identity prediction with regioselectivity ratio prediction; dropped 80C step; reframed non-specificity as evolutionary insight; removed mischaracterized PMID 31836519

═══════════════════════════════════════════
HYPOTHESIS: Abiotic Fenton Regioselectivity Fingerprint as Evidence for Evolutionary Refinement of 15-LOX
═══════════════════════════════════════════
CONNECTION: Ferroptosis (15-LOX regiospecific PLOOH generation) →→ Fenton radical non-selectivity vs enzymatic selectivity →→ Serpentinization (abiotic non-selective PLOOH generation on ferrihydrite surfaces)
CONFIDENCE: 5 — The chemical prediction is robust (non-selective radical chemistry is textbook); the evolutionary interpretation is plausible but untestable in its historical claims.
NOVELTY: Novel — No study has compared regioselectivity fingerprints of enzymatic (15-LOX) vs abiotic (ferrihydrite-Fenton) PUFA oxidation to infer evolutionary refinement of ferroptotic machinery.
GROUNDEDNESS: 6 — Core chemistry (Fenton non-selectivity, 15-LOX C15 specificity) is well-established. Novel prediction is the quantitative ratio comparison.
IMPACT IF TRUE: Medium-High — Would establish that ferroptosis execution evolved from a non-selective abiotic precursor, providing a chemical fossil record of antioxidant evolution.

MECHANISM
In ferroptosis, the enzyme 15-lipoxygenase (ALOX15) oxidizes arachidonic acid (AA, C20:4) esterified at the sn-2 position of phosphatidylethanolamine with >95% regioselectivity at the C15 position, producing 15-HpETE-PE — the primary ferroptotic death signal [GROUNDED: Kagan et al., Nat Chem Biol 2017; Kuhn et al., Biochim Biophys Acta 2015 on ALOX15 regioselectivity]. This extreme positional selectivity concentrates oxidative damage at the most membrane-destabilizing position (C15 oxidation maximally disrupts the acyl chain packing geometry in the bilayer midplane).

In contrast, hydroxyl radical (HO•) generated by Fenton chemistry (Fe2+ + H2O2 → Fe3+ + HO• + OH-) attacks PUFA bis-allylic hydrogens with near-equal probability at all susceptible positions. For arachidonic acid (C20:4), the four bis-allylic positions (C7, C10, C13, C16 — actually C7, C10, C13 between the four double bonds) and the two allylic positions would be attacked with selectivity governed only by bond dissociation energy differences (~75 kcal/mol for bis-allylic C-H, ~88 kcal/mol for allylic C-H) [GROUNDED: Howard & Ingold, Can J Chem 1967; Porter et al., Chem Res Toxicol 1995 on radical chain mechanisms]. The abiotic Fenton fingerprint should therefore show approximately equal oxidation at all bis-allylic positions, producing a characteristic FLAT regioselectivity profile.

The hypothesis proposes a bench-top experiment: expose phospholipid vesicles (GUVs containing PUFA-PE) to ferrihydrite-catalyzed Fenton chemistry at 37C and analyze the PLOOH product distribution by LC-MS/MS. The abiotic fingerprint (approximately equal C7:C10:C13:C15 oxidation, ratio ~1:1:1:1) should be quantitatively distinguishable from the enzymatic ferroptotic fingerprint (>95% C15, ratio ~0:0:0:20).

The NON-SPECIFICITY of abiotic Fenton is the key insight: evolution did not invent lipid peroxidation — it inherited the Fenton reaction from prebiotic geochemistry. What evolution invented was REGIOSELECTIVITY. 15-LOX evolved to concentrate oxidative attack at the single most membrane-destabilizing position (C15), converting a diffuse abiotic threat into a focused biological weapon/signal. This is analogous to how restriction enzymes evolved sequence specificity from non-specific nucleases.

Temperature series: 25C, 37C, 45C (staying below 50C to preserve ferrihydrite phase stability [GROUNDED: Cornell & Schwertmann, The Iron Oxides, 2003 — ferrihydrite transforms to hematite above ~60C]). The Arrhenius activation energy for radical H-abstraction (~30-35 kJ/mol) predicts minimal change in positional selectivity across this range, because BDE differences between bis-allylic positions are negligible. This temperature-independence of the flat profile is itself a falsifiable prediction.

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE
• From Ferroptosis: ALOX15 produces >95% 15-HpETE from arachidonic acid [GROUNDED: Kuhn et al., BBA 2015]. 15-HpETE-PE is the primary ferroptotic death signal [GROUNDED: Kagan et al., Nat Chem Biol 2017].
• From Geochemistry: Ferrihydrite is a potent heterogeneous Fenton catalyst at circumneutral pH [GROUNDED: Petigara et al., Environ Sci Technol 2002 on ferrihydrite-catalyzed H2O2 decomposition]. Rate constants for heterogeneous Fenton on iron oxide surfaces are well-characterized in environmental chemistry [GROUNDED: Kwan & Voelker, Environ Sci Technol 2003].
• Bridge: Non-selective HO• radical chemistry is textbook [GROUNDED: Buxton et al., J Phys Chem Ref Data 1988 on HO• rate constants]. The contrast between enzymatic and non-enzymatic lipid oxidation regioselectivity is established in the lipid peroxidation field but has never been framed as evidence for evolutionary refinement.

COUNTER-EVIDENCE & RISKS
• Ferrihydrite-catalyzed Fenton at pH 7.4 may produce ferryl ion (FeIV=O) rather than free HO• [GROUNDED: Hug & Leupin, Environ Sci Technol 2003]. Ferryl has different selectivity than HO• — it may show some positional preference, which would partially blur the "flat profile" prediction. This needs to be tested; if ferryl shows >2:1 selectivity at any position, the clean dichotomy is weakened.
• At physiological pH, Fe(II) availability is limited by precipitation. The experiment must use freshly prepared ferrihydrite suspensions with controlled H2O2 addition to maintain Fenton cycling.
• The evolutionary narrative (15-LOX evolved FROM non-selective Fenton) is an inference — the experiment demonstrates the chemical contrast but cannot prove the evolutionary path.

HOW TO TEST
1. Prepare GUVs containing 1-palmitoyl-2-arachidonoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine (PAPE) in DOPC matrix (30:70 mol ratio)
2. Expose to ferrihydrite nanoparticles (0.1 mg/mL, ~6 nm, synthesized by Fe(NO3)3 titration at pH 7) + 100 uM H2O2 at 37C for 2 hours
3. Extract lipids and analyze by LC-MS/MS with PLOOH-specific MRM transitions for positional isomers (5-HpETE-PE, 8-HpETE-PE, 11-HpETE-PE, 12-HpETE-PE, 15-HpETE-PE)
4. Parallel control: 15-LOX enzyme + same PAPE substrate
5. **Prediction (falsifiable)**: Fenton produces C5:C8:C11:C12:C15-HpETE ratios within 0.5-2.0 (flat profile). 15-LOX produces C15:all-others ratio >20:1. Temperature series (25C, 37C, 45C) shows <10% change in Fenton positional ratios.
6. **If FALSE**: Fenton shows >3:1 selectivity at any position → hypothesis of non-selective abiotic precursor is wrong. If 15-LOX shows <10:1 C15 selectivity → literature claims of regioselectivity are overstated.
7. Effort: Moderate — standard lipidomics lab, 4-6 months. Requires LC-MS/MS with isomer resolution capability.

## E2: PHREEQC Speciation Snapshots Predict the Dominant Fenton-Active Iron Complex During GSH Depletion
**Lineage**: H4 (PHREEQC Iron Speciation) → SPECIFICATION
**Key improvement**: Pivoted from LIP dynamics to speciation snapshots; dropped ferrihydrite dissolution prediction; added explicit crowding correction; focused on Fe-ligand complex dominance

═══════════════════════════════════════════
HYPOTHESIS: PHREEQC Speciation Snapshots Predict the Dominant Fenton-Active Iron Complex During GSH Depletion
═══════════════════════════════════════════
CONNECTION: Ferroptosis (iron speciation determines Fenton activity) →→ Geochemical equilibrium speciation modeling →→ Serpentinization geochemistry (PHREEQC speciation code)
CONFIDENCE: 5 — PHREEQC is validated for aqueous speciation; whether it captures intracellular iron speciation depends on the completeness of the thermodynamic database adapted for biological conditions.
NOVELTY: Novel — PHREEQC (USGS geochemical speciation code) has never been applied to any biological system. Zero precedent. This is a pure tool transfer.
GROUNDEDNESS: 5 — PHREEQC speciation capability is validated in geochemistry [GROUNDED: Parkhurst & Appelo, USGS PHREEQC documentation]. Biological iron speciation data exists for validation [GROUNDED: Hider & Kong, BioMetals 2013 on intracellular iron speciation]. The gap is whether the adapted thermodynamic database will be complete enough.
IMPACT IF TRUE: Medium — Would add speciation-level resolution to ferroptosis models, identifying which iron complex (Fe-GSH, Fe-citrate, Fe-ADP, Fe-phosphate) drives Fenton chemistry at each stage of GSH depletion.

MECHANISM
The labile iron pool (LIP) in mammalian cells consists not of free Fe2+ but of iron complexed with various low-molecular-weight ligands: glutathione (Fe-GSH), citrate (Fe-citrate), ADP/ATP (Fe-ADP), phosphate (Fe-HPO4), and amino acids [GROUNDED: Hider & Kong, BioMetals 2013; Kakhlon & Cabantchik, Free Radic Biol Med 2002]. Each Fe-ligand complex has different Fenton activity: Fe-citrate and Fe-ADP are more Fenton-active than Fe-GSH, because GSH's thiol coordinates Fe2+ in a geometry that reduces H2O2 access [GROUNDED: Aust et al., Free Radic Biol Med 1985; Engelmann et al., BioMetals 2003 on differential Fenton activity of Fe complexes].

During ferroptosis initiation by erastin (which depletes GSH via system Xc- inhibition), the GSH concentration drops from ~5 mM to <0.5 mM [GROUNDED: Dixon et al., Cell 2012]. This fundamentally reshuffles the iron speciation equilibrium: Fe-GSH complexes dissociate, and iron redistributes to citrate, ADP, and phosphate complexes. The hypothesis proposes that this speciation shift — from the relatively Fenton-inactive Fe-GSH to Fenton-active Fe-citrate and Fe-ADP — is itself a significant amplifier of ferroptotic Fenton chemistry, independent of GPX4 inhibition.

PHREEQC (pH-REdox-EQuilibrium in C; USGS) solves multi-component aqueous speciation equilibria using thermodynamic databases [GROUNDED: Parkhurst & Appelo, USGS 2013]. It is the standard tool in geochemistry for predicting which mineral or aqueous species dominates under given conditions of pH, Eh, temperature, and total element concentrations. The hypothesis proposes adapting PHREEQC to model intracellular iron speciation by:

1. Constructing a custom PHREEQC database with stability constants for Fe-GSH, Fe-citrate, Fe-ADP, Fe-phosphate, Fe-amino acid complexes (literature values available for most [GROUNDED: NIST Critically Selected Stability Constants database])
2. Defining the intracellular solution: pH 7.2, Eh -300 mV, T = 37C, total Fe = 1 uM (total LIP), GSH = 5 mM (normal) or 0.5 mM (erastin-depleted), citrate = 0.3 mM, ATP = 3 mM, phosphate = 1 mM [GROUNDED: approximate intracellular concentrations from Alberts et al., Molecular Biology of the Cell]
3. Running speciation snapshots at GSH = 5, 3, 1, 0.5, 0.1 mM to predict the Fe-ligand redistribution trajectory

CRITICAL LIMITATION: Macromolecular crowding (20-30% w/v protein in cytoplasm) reduces effective concentrations by altering activity coefficients. PHREEQC uses Pitzer or SIT models for ionic strength corrections, but these are parameterized for geological brines, not protein-crowded solutions. A correction factor of 2-5x for effective concentrations must be applied [PARAMETRIC: estimate based on known crowding effects on reaction rates, Zhou et al., Annu Rev Biophys 2008]. This introduces systematic uncertainty but does not invalidate the speciation ranking (the RELATIVE dominance of Fe-citrate vs Fe-GSH should be preserved even with a global correction factor).

NOTE: This hypothesis does NOT model LIP dynamics over time (the LIP does NOT expand during ferroptosis per PMC12236665, July 2025 [GROUNDED]). It models STATIC speciation equilibria at discrete GSH depletion states — answering "which Fe complex dominates at each GSH level?" not "how does total LIP change?"

NOTE: Ferritin dissolution is NOT modeled. Ferritin core dissolution is protein-shell-mediated (ferritinophagy, lysosomal proteolysis) and has fundamentally different kinetics from open mineral surface dissolution. PHREEQC could model the speciation of iron AFTER release from ferritin, but not the ferritin dissolution process itself.

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE
• From Ferroptosis: GSH depletion by erastin shifts iron speciation [GROUNDED: Dixon et al., Cell 2012]. Different Fe complexes have different Fenton activities [GROUNDED: Engelmann et al., BioMetals 2003].
• From Geochemistry: PHREEQC is the gold standard for aqueous speciation modeling, freely available (USGS), validated against thousands of geochemical systems [GROUNDED: Parkhurst & Appelo, 2013].
• Bridge: Aqueous iron speciation obeys the same thermodynamics in cells and in geology. Stability constants are transferable [GROUNDED: NIST database].

COUNTER-EVIDENCE & RISKS
• Macromolecular crowding introduces 2-5x systematic error in effective concentrations. The Pitzer model in PHREEQC is not designed for this.
• The NIST stability constants are measured in dilute solution at 25C. At 37C and high ionic strength (~150 mM), values may shift.
• Protein-bound iron (ferritin, mitochondrial ISCs, heme) is not captured by PHREEQC — only the truly labile fraction.
• The practical improvement over existing ODE-based ferroptosis models is uncertain. Ferroptosis sensitivity is dominated by GPX4 activity and ACSL4-mediated PUFA-PE enrichment — iron speciation may be a secondary effect.

HOW TO TEST
1. Build PHREEQC input file with intracellular conditions (pH 7.2, Eh -300 mV, 37C, concentrations above)
2. Run speciation snapshots at GSH = 5, 3, 1, 0.5, 0.1 mM
3. **Prediction (falsifiable)**: At GSH = 5 mM, Fe-GSH dominates (>60% of LIP). At GSH < 1 mM, Fe-citrate dominates (>40%). The crossover point occurs at GSH ~2 mM. This speciation shift increases total Fenton activity by >3-fold (because Fe-citrate is ~5x more Fenton-active than Fe-GSH [GROUNDED: Engelmann et al., 2003]).
4. **Experimental validation**: Measure Fenton activity (H2O2 consumption rate, HO• production by fluorescent probe) in cell lysate as a function of added GSH at 0.1-5 mM. Compare measured activity curve with PHREEQC-predicted speciation shift.
5. **If FALSE**: Fenton activity does not change with GSH concentration → speciation shift is not functionally relevant. If Fe-GSH is NOT the dominant complex at 5 mM GSH → thermodynamic database is incomplete or wrong.
6. Effort: Low — PHREEQC is free software, database construction ~1 month, cell lysate Fenton assays ~2 months. Total: 3-4 months.

## E3: Pourbaix-Guided Ferrihydrite PLOOH Experiment — Thermodynamic Roadmap for Abiotic Lipid Peroxidation
**Lineage**: H2 × H5 → CROSSOVER
**Key improvement**: Combines H2's wet-lab PLOOH experiment with H5's Pourbaix framework. The Pourbaix diagram predicts WHICH iron oxidation state and species dominates under each experimental condition, providing a thermodynamic roadmap that tells you where to look for maximum Fenton activity.

═══════════════════════════════════════════
HYPOTHESIS: Pourbaix-Guided Ferrihydrite PLOOH Experiment — Thermodynamic Roadmap for Abiotic Lipid Peroxidation
═══════════════════════════════════════════
CONNECTION: Ferroptosis (ferritin core = ferrihydrite, PLOOHs as death signal) →→ Pourbaix-predicted iron speciation fields + Fenton PLOOH generation →→ Serpentinization (iron mineral phase transitions + Pourbaix diagram framework)
CONFIDENCE: 5 — The Pourbaix framework is rigorous and the PLOOH detection is feasible. The crossover adds value: instead of blindly varying pH and Eh, the Pourbaix diagram tells you exactly where maximum Fenton activity should occur.
NOVELTY: Novel — No study has used Pourbaix diagrams to design a lipid peroxidation experiment. The integration of geochemical thermodynamic prediction with ferroptosis-relevant lipidomics is entirely new.
GROUNDEDNESS: 6 — Pourbaix diagrams for iron are textbook [GROUNDED: Pourbaix, Atlas of Electrochemical Equilibria, 1974]. Ferritin core = ferrihydrite is established [GROUNDED: Harrison & Arosio, BBA 1996]. PLOOH detection by LC-MS/MS is routine in ferroptosis labs [GROUNDED: Kagan et al., 2017].
IMPACT IF TRUE: Medium-High — Would validate geochemical thermodynamic modeling as a design tool for ferroptosis experiments, and establish that ferritin-derived ferrihydrite Fenton activity is predictable from first-principles Pourbaix analysis.

MECHANISM
The ferritin core IS ferrihydrite (5Fe2O3·9H2O) — a nanocrystalline iron oxyhydroxide mineral identical to that formed during serpentinization [GROUNDED: Harrison & Arosio, BBA 1996; Pan et al., Biochemistry 2009 on ferritin core structure]. During ferritinophagy, the protein shell is degraded in lysosomes (pH 4.5-5.0), releasing the ferrihydrite core into an acidic environment, where it dissolves to Fe3+(aq) and is subsequently reduced to Fe2+(aq) upon release to the cytoplasm (pH 7.2, Eh ~ -300 mV) [GROUNDED: Gao et al., Autophagy 2016].

Pourbaix diagrams (pH-Eh stability diagrams) for the Fe-H2O system predict exactly which iron species dominates at every pH-Eh combination [GROUNDED: Pourbaix 1974; Beverskog & Puigdomenech, Corros Sci 1996 for refined Fe Pourbaix diagrams]:
- At pH 4.5, Eh +100 mV (lysosome): Fe3+(aq) dominates — consistent with dissolution
- At pH 7.2, Eh -300 mV (cytoplasm): Fe2+(aq) dominates — Fenton-active
- At pH 7.2, Eh +200 mV (after GPX4 inhibition shifts redox?): Ferrihydrite is stable — Fenton quenched

The Pourbaix diagram therefore predicts a WINDOW of maximum Fenton activity: the pH-Eh region where Fe2+(aq) is the stable species AND H2O2 coexists. This window has been precisely mapped in environmental geochemistry but never applied to intracellular conditions.

The crossover experiment: Construct the Pourbaix diagram for Fe at 37C with relevant chelators (citrate at 0.3 mM). Then prepare a matrix of lipid peroxidation experiments spanning the predicted Fenton-active window:
- pH 4.5, 5.5, 6.5, 7.2, 8.0
- Eh -400 to +200 mV (controlled by reductant/oxidant ratio)
- Ferrihydrite nanoparticles as iron source (mimicking ferritin core)
- PUFA-PE vesicles as substrate

At each pH-Eh point, measure PLOOH production rate by LC-MS/MS. The Pourbaix diagram predicts where the PLOOH production rate should peak (at the boundary between Fe2+ and ferrihydrite stability fields, where dissolving ferrihydrite provides a continuous Fe2+ flux). The hypothesis is that the MEASURED PLOOH rate map will correspond to the PREDICTED Pourbaix Fe2+ stability field with >80% spatial overlap.

This design principle — use a geochemical Pourbaix diagram to predict the optimal conditions for a biological process — represents a genuine methodological transfer from Earth science to cell biology.

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE
• From Ferroptosis: Ferritinophagy releases iron from ferrihydrite cores → LIP → Fenton → PLOOHs [GROUNDED: Gao et al., 2016; Kagan et al., 2017].
• From Geochemistry: Pourbaix diagrams quantitatively predict iron speciation as a function of pH and Eh [GROUNDED: Pourbaix 1974; Beverskog & Puigdomenech, 1996].
• Bridge: Ferritin core = ferrihydrite. Same mineral, same thermodynamics. The Pourbaix diagram applies to both geological and biological ferrihydrite.

COUNTER-EVIDENCE & RISKS
• Chelators (citrate, GSH, phosphate) shift Pourbaix stability field boundaries. The "pure Fe" Pourbaix diagram would need modification. This is feasible but adds complexity [GROUNDED: chelator-modified Pourbaix diagrams exist for environmental applications, e.g., Stumm & Morgan, Aquatic Chemistry, 1996].
• Kinetic effects may cause the observed PLOOH map to deviate from thermodynamic predictions — metastable Fe species could persist outside their equilibrium stability fields.
• At physiological pH 7.2, the Fenton mechanism may shift from HO• to ferryl (FeIV=O), which has different reactivity [GROUNDED: Hug & Leupin, EST 2003].

HOW TO TEST
1. Construct Pourbaix diagram for Fe-H2O-citrate system at 37C using PHREEQC (connects to E2)
2. Prepare 5x5 matrix of experimental conditions: pH (4.5, 5.5, 6.5, 7.2, 8.0) × Eh (-400, -200, 0, +100, +200 mV)
3. At each point: ferrihydrite NPs (0.1 mg/mL) + PAPE vesicles + appropriate Eh-poising buffer. Measure PLOOH by LC-MS/MS at 2 hours.
4. **Prediction (falsifiable)**: PLOOH production rate map shows >80% spatial overlap with Pourbaix-predicted Fe2+(aq) stability field. Maximum PLOOH production at pH 5.5-6.5, Eh -100 to 0 mV (the boundary where ferrihydrite dissolves to Fe2+). PLOOH production drops by >10-fold outside the Fe2+ field.
5. **If FALSE**: <50% overlap → intracellular conditions are too complex for Pourbaix prediction (kinetics dominate over thermodynamics). If PLOOH production peaks far from predicted Fe2+ field → the Fenton mechanism at these conditions is not Fe2+-mediated.
6. Effort: Moderate-High — requires Eh-controlled reaction vessels (potentiostat or chemical poising), LC-MS/MS, Pourbaix diagram computation. 6-9 months. Collaboration between environmental geochemistry and lipidomics labs.

## E4: Dissolution-Dependent Fenton Activity Curve of Ferritin-Derived Ferrihydrite Nanoparticles
**Lineage**: H8 (Nanoparticle Surface-Area Scaling) → MUTATION
**Key improvement**: Dropped the etch-pit geological model (invalid at 6-8 nm scale); refocused on the directly measurable quantity (Fenton activity per Fe atom as a function of dissolution fraction); incorporated ferritin-as-evolved-containment-of-Fenton-reactor concept from critic

═══════════════════════════════════════════
HYPOTHESIS: Dissolution-Dependent Fenton Activity Curve of Ferritin-Derived Ferrihydrite Nanoparticles
═══════════════════════════════════════════
CONNECTION: Ferroptosis (ferritinophagy releases iron for Fenton chemistry) →→ Ferrihydrite nanoparticle dissolution kinetics →→ Serpentinization (mineral dissolution surface-area-dependent catalysis)
CONFIDENCE: 5 — The measurement is feasible and the question (does Fenton activity per Fe atom change during ferrihydrite dissolution?) has a definite answer. The geochemical analogy provides testable predictions but may not apply at the 6-8 nm scale.
NOVELTY: Novel — No study has measured Fenton activity as a function of ferrihydrite nanoparticle dissolution fraction. Environmental chemistry measures bulk dissolution; ferroptosis biology measures total iron — neither measures the dissolution-activity relationship.
GROUNDEDNESS: 6 — Ferrihydrite Fenton catalysis is well-characterized in environmental chemistry [GROUNDED: Kwan & Voelker, EST 2003]. Ferritin core = ferrihydrite [GROUNDED: Harrison & Arosio, BBA 1996]. The gap is the nanoparticle-specific dissolution-activity curve at the 6-8 nm scale.
IMPACT IF TRUE: Medium — Would reveal whether ferritin functions as an evolved containment vessel for a geochemical Fenton reactor, where the protein shell controls iron release kinetics (and thus Fenton activity) more precisely than bare mineral dissolution could.

MECHANISM
Ferritin stores up to ~4500 Fe atoms as a 6-8 nm ferrihydrite nanoparticle core inside a 24-subunit protein cage [GROUNDED: Harrison & Arosio, BBA 1996]. During ferritinophagy, this core is released and dissolves, providing iron for Fenton chemistry. The question this hypothesis addresses: how does Fenton activity change as the ferrihydrite nanoparticle progressively dissolves?

In environmental geochemistry, mineral dissolution kinetics are surface-area-dependent: the rate of Fe release from ferrihydrite scales with reactive surface area (RSA), which changes non-linearly during dissolution [GROUNDED: Cornell & Schwertmann, The Iron Oxides, 2003; Schwertmann et al., Clay Minerals 2004 on ferrihydrite dissolution kinetics]. For larger particles (>100 nm), dissolution can proceed via etch pit formation, creating complex surface area trajectories [GROUNDED: Luttge, J Electron Spectrosc Relat Phenom 2006 on mineral dissolution mechanisms]. However, at the 6-8 nm scale of ferritin cores, the entire particle is essentially "surface" — there is no bulk interior for etch pits to form. Dissolution at this scale proceeds via surface retreat (shrinking sphere model) [GROUNDED: theoretical expectation for nanoparticles below ~10 nm].

The key insight from the geochemistry literature: the Fenton catalytic activity of ferrihydrite depends not just on total Fe released but on the surface Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio and the density of reactive surface sites [GROUNDED: Kwan & Voelker, EST 2003; Pham et al., EST 2012 on surface-mediated Fenton mechanism at iron oxide surfaces]. As a nanoparticle dissolves, the Fe2+/Fe3+ surface ratio changes, and so does the per-atom Fenton activity. The dissolution-dependent Fenton activity curve is therefore NOT simply linear with dissolved Fe — it should show a characteristic shape determined by surface chemistry.

The hypothesis predicts three possible dissolution-activity curve shapes:
(A) LINEAR: Fenton activity ∝ dissolved Fe. No surface effect. Boring but testable.
(B) ACCELERATING: Fenton activity increases faster than dissolved Fe. This would occur if dissolution exposes increasingly reactive (Fe2+-rich) surface sites — consistent with a "reactive core" model where the outer surface is more oxidized.
(C) DECELERATING: Fenton activity increases slower than dissolved Fe at high dissolution fractions. This would occur if the most reactive surface sites dissolve first.

The ferritin-as-evolved-Fenton-containment concept (from the critic): If ferritin evolved to CONTAIN a geochemical Fenton reactor, the protein shell is not just an iron storage device — it is a KINETIC BARRIER that prevents the Fenton reaction from running uncontrolled. The bare ferrihydrite nanoparticle without the protein shell should have HIGHER Fenton activity than the same particle inside ferritin, because the shell restricts H2O2 access and Fe2+ release. This predicts a measurable difference: Fenton activity of bare ferrihydrite NPs > Fenton activity of intact ferritin at the same Fe loading.

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE
• From Ferroptosis: Ferritinophagy is a key driver of ferroptosis [GROUNDED: Gao et al., Autophagy 2016; Hou et al., Autophagy 2016]. Ferritin sequesters iron to prevent Fenton chemistry [GROUNDED: Arosio et al., BBA 2009].
• From Geochemistry: Ferrihydrite dissolution kinetics and surface-area-dependent catalysis are well-characterized [GROUNDED: Schwertmann et al., 2004; Kwan & Voelker, 2003]. Surface Fe2+ sites are the primary Fenton-active species on iron oxide surfaces [GROUNDED: Pham et al., EST 2012].
• Bridge: Ferritin core = ferrihydrite. The protein shell is an evolved kinetic barrier around a geochemical catalyst.

COUNTER-EVIDENCE & RISKS
• At 6-8 nm, the "shrinking sphere" model may oversimplify dissolution. Amorphous ferrihydrite may dissolve more uniformly than crystalline minerals, making the activity curve trivially linear (outcome A).
• The protein shell of ferritin has channels that selectively regulate Fe2+ access [GROUNDED: Theil, Annu Rev Biochem 2004 on ferritin iron channels]. This makes the bare NP vs intact ferritin comparison meaningful, but also means biological regulation may overwhelm the mineral dissolution kinetics.
• Fenton activity measurement at nanoparticle concentrations requires sensitive detection methods (fluorescent probes, EPR spin trapping) that have their own artefacts.

HOW TO TEST
1. Synthesize 6-nm ferrihydrite nanoparticles by established protocols (Fe(NO3)3 hydrolysis at pH 7 [GROUNDED: Schwertmann & Cornell, Iron Oxides in the Laboratory, 2000])
2. Partial dissolution series: dissolve to 0%, 10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% using reductive dissolution (ascorbate at pH 3) or acidic dissolution (pH 2 HCl). Measure dissolved Fe by ferrozine assay.
