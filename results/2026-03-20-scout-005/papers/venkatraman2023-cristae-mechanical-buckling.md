# Cristae Formation Is a Mechanical Buckling Event Controlled by the Inner Mitochondrial Membrane Lipidome

**Authors:** Venkatraman, Lee, Garcia, Mahapatra, Milshteyn, Perkins, Kim, Pasolli, Phan, Lippincott-Schwartz, Ellisman, Rangamani, Budin
**Year:** 2023/2024
**Journal:** The EMBO Journal
**DOI/URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.15252/embj.2023114054 | PMC10711667
**Status:** Peer-reviewed

---

## Abstract

Combines experimental lipidome dissection with multi-scale modeling to show how lipid interactions control inner mitochondrial membrane (IMM) morphology. Finds cardiolipin specifically buffers against curvature loss, independent of ATP synthase dimerization, and a "snapthrough instability" drives IMM collapse with small membrane property changes.

---

## Key Findings

**Snapthrough Instability:**
- Discontinuous (first-order-like) transition in cristae morphology as lipid composition changes continuously
- Small changes in membrane parameters cause abrupt jumps between morphological states (shallow invagination vs. extended tubule)
- Mathematically equivalent to a bifurcation / first-order phase transition — but NOT framed as such

**Theoretical Framework:**
- Continuum modeling framework treating cristae tubules as axisymmetric tubes budding from flat membrane
- Anisotropic curvature from ATP synthase: D₀ parameter
- Isotropic membrane curvature from asymmetrically localized cardiolipin: C₀ parameter
- Localized collar force density ~8 pN/nm at tubule neck
- Variable membrane stiffness: 10–20 kBT bending modulus
- Variable membrane tension: 0.01 pN/nm

**Cardiolipin Role:**
- CL specifically buffers IMM against curvature loss
- Effect is independent of ATP synthase dimerization
- Mechanism: CL contribution to C₀ (isotropic spontaneous curvature) shifts the bifurcation point

**What the Paper Does NOT Do:**
- Does NOT use Helfrich formalism explicitly (no κ_G / Gaussian curvature modulus)
- Does NOT invoke liquid crystal theory or order parameters
- Does NOT frame "snapthrough instability" as an LC first-order phase transition
- Does NOT use packing parameter language
- Gaussian curvature modulus absent from the model entirely

---

## Why Selected

Most important theoretical paper on cristae shape mechanics. The "snapthrough instability" is structurally identical to a first-order LC phase transition — discontinuous jump at a critical parameter value — but the connection to LC theory has never been made. This is the primary novelty gap: reframing the Venkatraman snapthrough as a first-order lyotropic LC phase transition governed by CL packing parameter would unify the mechanical buckling framework with LC theory and yield new predictions about the Gaussian curvature modulus as the key missing parameter.
